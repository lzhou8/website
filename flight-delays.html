Flight Delays: An Exploratory Data Analysis

Introduction:

From May to August 2019, I interned at a U.S. airline and had the opportunity of exploring the world through standby travel. Being a frequent flyer this summer, I've experienced flight delays ranging from less than an hour to a couple hours. This inspired me to create some data visualizations to gain key insights behind flight delays.

Data:

I used d3.js to read 12 datasets (in CSV format) provided by the Bureau of Transportation Statistics. Each dataset contains millions of flight delay records for over 15+ airlines in a certain calendar month. Within each dataset, each record contains statistics such as origin airport, destination airport, number of delay minutes, and more.

Since I wanted to extract relevant statistics for 4 specific airlines (Delta, American, United, and Southwest), I decided to first preprocess the data by using d3â€™s built-in CSV and queue functions to read in and reformat data from multiple CSV files. Once preprocessed, the data was then used to build 2 different visualizations: 1) an interactive line chart in d3.js to visualize the overall trends in flight delays in 2018 for 4 airlines, and 2) a bar chart to visualize 5 different causes of flight delays.

Analysis:

This first visualization seeks to answer the following questions:

Which of the 4 airlines had the most flight delays in 2018?
What are some observable trends in flight delays in 2018?

An interesting observation here is that Southwest Airlines had the highest number of flight delays across the board. In July 2018, Southwest reached its peak of 1,913,938 flight delays - the highest reported number within the year. Additionally, the data seems to suggest a general increase in flight delays during the summer months for all 4 airlines. This phenomenon could be explained by an increase in travel in the summer.

The second visualization seeks to answer the following question: What were the causes of flight delays in 2018?

According to the 12 datasets, 5 different causes of flight delays have been reported: carrier, weather, NAS (National Air System), security, or late aircraft. I found this link to be helpful in understanding their definitions.


Interestingly, but perhaps unsurprisingly, weather is the #1 reason behind flight delays for all 4 airlines. Carrier delays make up the #2 reason, which include cleaning/maintenance of the aircraft and cargo loading.

The third and fourth reasons are delays incurred by NAS (National Air System) and late aircraft arrivals. NAS is responsible for maintaining optimal air traffic conditions; thus a flight would be delayed if there is heavy air traffic. Late aircraft arrival delays are caused by the late arrival of the preceding aircraft, causing a propagation effect for the next aircraft to depart late.

Security delays represent the most infrequent reason behind flight delays across all 4 airlines. These delays result from intruder breaches or terminal evacuations, which are rare occurrences.
